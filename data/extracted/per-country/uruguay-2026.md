## Personal Income Tax

Uruguay uses a **cedular (schedular) system** called IRPF (Impuesto a la Renta de las Personas Físicas), effective from 1 July 2007. Income is divided into two categories taxed separately:

- **Category I (Capital):** Capital gains and investment income — flat 12% rate
- **Category II (Labor):** Income from dependent or independent work — progressive rates 0%–36%

Tax year = calendar year. Residents taxed on Uruguayan-source income (and some foreign-source income under Category I conditions). Non-residents: taxed at 12% on Uruguayan-source income.

Exchange rate reference: **UYU 41 = USD 1** (EY p. 1687)

**Residency:** Physical presence for more than 183 days in calendar year; OR center of vital economic interests in Uruguay (principal asset base); OR acquisition of habitual abode.

### Category I — Capital Income Rates

| Income type | Rate |
|---|---|
| Capital gains, dividends, interest, rental income | 12% |
| Non-resident individuals (Uruguayan-source) | 12% |

**Notes:**
- Real estate transfer tax: 4% (2% seller + 2% buyer) on property sales (EY p. 1688)
- Net worth tax: 0.1% on net assets per year (for individuals owning assets in Uruguay); tax-free allowance UYU 6,381,000 (2024; 2025 to be determined); for non-residents not subject to income tax: rates vary 0.7%–1.5% (EY p. 1688)
- Pension income: subject to Tax of Assistance to Social Security (similar to IRPF)
- Non-residents: 12% flat on Uruguayan-source income; also subject to technical services tax if income from technical services abroad meets certain conditions

## Social Security

Uruguay's social security system (BPS — Banco de Previsión Social) covers pension, health (FONASA), and other contributions (EY p. 1688):

| Contribution | Employee | Employer | Self-employed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pension fund (BPS) | 15% (a) | 7.5% (a) | Variable (c) |
| Medical care (FONASA) | 3%–8% (health-linked) | 5% | 3%–8% |
| Work Reconversion Fund | 0.1% | 0.1% | — |
| Labor Credits Guarantee Fund | — | 0.025% | — |
| **Total (approx. employee)** | **~18–23%** | **~12.6%** | **~18–23%** |

**(a)** Pension fund employee rate: 15% on income that does not exceed approximately UYU 256,821/month; higher income: mixed pillar system with AFAP (private pension fund)

**(b)** Work Reconversion and Labor Credits funds: paid to BPS (social security body)

**(c)** Self-employment contributions: calculated by deducting 30% from total amounts invoiced; pension fund contributions vary by category

**FONASA health contribution detail:**
- Employee: 3% base + additional percentage depending on whether the employee has dependants and income level; ranges 3%–8% typically
- Employer: flat 5% on gross salary

**Income ceiling:**
- Pension contributions ceiling: approximately UYU 256,821/month (the BPS-defined maximum; above this, contributions go to the AFAP private pillar)

**Notes:**
- BPS contributions are tax-deductible from IRPF taxable income (Category II)
- Totalization agreements in force with many countries (Argentina, Austria, Belgium, Bolivia, Brazil, Canada, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, El Salvador, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Israel, Italy, Japan, Korea, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Paraguay, Peru, Portugal, Quebec, Romania, Russian Federation (c), Spain, Switzerland, UAE, US, Venezuela — EY p. 1688)

## Self-Employment Regimes

### Option A — Independent Professional (IRPF Category II)

- **Who qualifies:** Independent professionals and freelancers providing services in Uruguay
- **Tax base:** Gross revenue invoiced; self-employment contributions deductible; BPS contributions calculated at 30% deemed expense ratio on gross invoiced amounts
- **Rate / mechanism:** Progressive IRPF Category II rates 0%–36% on net income after BPS deductions
- **Social security:** BPS contributions apply; pension fund + FONASA at equivalent rates to employees; calculated on 70% of gross invoiced amounts (after 30% deduction)
- **VAT obligations:** Must register for IVA (VAT) if providing services; standard rate 22%; reduced rate 10% for some goods/services
- **Key limits:** Annual tax return required; advance monthly payments via withholding or self-payment

### Option B — Small Taxpayer Regime (Monotributo)

- **Who qualifies:** Small-scale sole traders and micro-entrepreneurs with very limited turnover (below specific BPC-indexed thresholds); also social monotributo for informal workers joining the system
- **Tax base / Rate:** A single monthly contribution covers both social security (BPS) and income tax; amount varies by activity category
- **VAT obligations:** Simplified; reduced obligations
- **Key limits:** Strict turnover limits; excluded from standard IRPF and IVA obligations

### Option C — Sociedades Personales / Single-Member Company (IRAE)

- **Who qualifies:** Individuals structuring as a company (e.g., Sociedad de Responsabilidad Limitada); subject to IRAE (corporate income tax) at 25% instead of IRPF
- **Tax base:** Net business income under IRAE rules
- **Rate:** 25% IRAE; dividends distributed to individual owners subject to 7% withholding (Category I IRPF)
- **Social security:** Individual owner: same BPS obligations as self-employed
- **Key limits:** Corporate compliance obligations; only advantageous at high income levels where combined IRAE + dividend tax < top IRPF marginal rate

## Special Regimes for Newcomers / Expats

### New Tax Resident — Foreign Income Exemption (EY pp. 1687–1688)

- **Eligibility:** Individuals who acquire tax residency in Uruguay for the first time (including digital nomads and returnees)
- **Benefit:** During the year of becoming a tax resident and the following **ten years** (total up to 11 years), the individual may choose to:
  - Option 1: Pay a flat 7% IRPF (Category I rate) on all foreign-source income remitted/available in Uruguay, OR
  - Option 2: **Exempt all foreign-source income** from Uruguayan tax entirely
  - The "Option 2" choice effectively makes Uruguay a territorial tax jurisdiction for the first 11 years
- **Duration:** Year of acquisition of residency + 10 following years
- **Application:** Election made at time of first annual tax declaration; consult DGI for procedural requirements
- **Caveats:** Foreign-source passive income (dividends, interest, capital gains) not taxable; foreign-source labor income also not taxable if option 2 selected; after 11 years, worldwide income subject to normal rules

**This is the most significant regime for digital nomads and remote workers.** A person working remotely for a foreign company from Uruguay is typically exempt from Uruguayan tax on their labor income for up to 11 years.
