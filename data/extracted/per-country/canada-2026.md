## Personal Income Tax

Progressive system at both federal and provincial levels. Tax year = calendar year (1 Jan – 31 Dec).
Filing deadline: 30 April (T1 General); 15 June for self-employed individuals (but tax owing due 30 April).
Spouses taxed separately on all types of income — joint filing not permitted. (EY p.1234)
Federal tax applies across all provinces/territories; only Quebec collects its own income tax separately.
This file uses **Federal + Ontario** as the reference combined scenario. (EY p.1230)

### Federal income tax — 2026

The lowest federal rate was reduced from 15% to 14% effective 1 July 2025, resulting in an effective rate of 14.5% for the 2025 tax year, and **14% for 2026 and subsequent years**. Brackets indexed at 2.0% for 2026. (CRA 2026 / taxtips.ca)

| Taxable income (CAD) | Rate | Tax on lower amount (CAD) |
|---|---|---|
| $0 – $58,523 | 14% | $0 |
| $58,523 – $117,045 | 20.5% | $8,193 |
| $117,045 – $181,440 | 26% | $20,194 |
| $181,440 – $258,482 | 29% | $36,926 |
| Over $258,482 | 33% | $59,270 |

> Note: EY (p.1230) shows 2025 brackets (0–$57,375 at 14.5%; $57,375–$114,750 at 20.5%; $114,750–$177,882 at 26%; $177,882–$253,414 at 29%; $253,414+ at 33%). The 2026 brackets above reflect the 14% rate (full year) and 2% indexation confirmed by CRA T4127 Jan 2026 / taxtips.ca.

**Basic Personal Amount (BPA) 2026:** $16,452
- Comprises base amount ($14,829) + additional amount ($1,623)
- The additional amount phases out for net income between $181,440 and $258,482 (effectively a 29% bracket surtax effect in that range)
- Delivered as a non-refundable 14% federal tax credit = $2,303 credit (at minimum BPA)
(CRA 2026; taxtips.ca)

**Additional federal personal tax credits (non-refundable, 2025 amounts; indexed):** (EY p.1229)
- Spousal credit (equal to BPA if spouse has no income): up to $2,303
- Employment credit: $1,433 (2025) — available to all employees
- Age credit: $7,898 (2025), reduced for income over $44,325 (2025)
- Disability credit: $9,428 (2025)
- Canada Caregiver credit: various amounts
- Charitable donations: 15% federal credit on first $200; 29% (or 33% if top bracket) on amounts over $200

**Capital gains inclusion rate:** 50% of capital gains included in taxable income (EY p.1226). Capital losses deductible only against capital gains; unused losses carried back 3 years or forward indefinitely.

**Capital gains exemption:**
- Qualifying small business corporation shares: lifetime $1,250,000 (for dispositions on/after 25 June 2024) (EY p.1227)
- Qualifying farm/fishing property: lifetime $1,250,000

**Dividends:**
- Eligible dividends (from public companies): 138% gross-up; federal dividend tax credit ~20.73% of cash amount
- Ineligible dividends: 115% gross-up; federal dividend tax credit ~10.38% of cash amount
(EY p.1223)

**TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account):** Annual contribution limit CAD $7,000 (2024 and subsequent years). Investment earnings tax-free; withdrawals not taxable. (EY p.1224)

**RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan):** Annual limit: lesser of 18% of prior-year earned income and $32,490 (2025). Contributions deductible; withdrawals taxable. (EY p.1229)

**AMT (Alternative Minimum Tax):** Flat 20.5% on alternative minimum taxable income; blanket CAD $177,882 exemption for 2025 (indexed). (EY p.1231)

**Standard deductions / allowances:**
- Employee: limited deductible expenses (RPP contributions, union/professional dues, certain travel for commission employees, moving expenses)
- No equivalent of a broad US-style standard deduction; tax credits used instead
- Business/investment interest deductible if borrowed to earn income
- Home office expenses: deductible for employees (simplified flat rate or detailed method)
(EY p.1228–1229)

**Notes:**
- Quebec residents file a separate provincial return; all other provinces/territories file one combined federal/provincial T1 return
- Income attribution rules prevent splitting income with spouses/minor children to access lower brackets

### Ontario Provincial Income Tax — 2026

Ontario has 5 provincial brackets plus a surtax on higher provincial tax amounts. Ontario brackets indexed at 1.9% for 2026 (except the $150,000 and $220,000 thresholds, which are not indexed). (taxtips.ca; CRA T4032-ON 2026)

| Taxable income (CAD) | Ontario Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $53,891 | 5.05% |
| $53,891 – $107,785 | 9.15% |
| $107,785 – $150,000 | 11.16% |
| $150,000 – $220,000 | 12.16% |
| Over $220,000 | 13.16% |

**Ontario Basic Personal Amount 2026:** ~$12,440 (indexed ~1.9% from $12,210 in 2025).

**Ontario surtax (2026):** Applied on top of basic Ontario tax:
- 20% surtax on Ontario tax exceeding ~$5,315
- An additional 36% surtax on Ontario tax exceeding ~$6,802
- Combined surtax pushes the effective top Ontario marginal rate to ~20.08% (13.16% × 1.56)
- Top combined federal + Ontario marginal rate: 53.53% (EY p.1231)

**Top combined rates (federal + Ontario, 2025 basis):** (EY p.1231)
- Ordinary income: 53.53%
- Eligible dividends: 39.34%
- Non-eligible dividends: 47.74%
- Capital gains: 26.77%

## Social Security

### Canada Pension Plan (CPP)

Employers and employees each contribute to CPP at the same rate. Self-employed pay both sides. (EY p.1232)

| Contribution | Employee | Employer | Self-employed |
|---|---|---|---|
| CPP (base, 2025) | 5.95% | 5.95% | 11.90% |
| CPP2 (second additional, 2025) | up to 4%* | up to 4%* | up to 8%* |
| **Total CPP (approx., 2025)** | **~5.95–8.0%** | **~5.95–8.0%** | **~11.9–16.0%** |

*CPP2: 4% rate applies to earnings between the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings ($71,300 in 2025) and the Year's Additional Maximum Pensionable Earnings ($81,200 in 2025). (EY p.1232–1233)

**CPP 2025 key figures:** (EY p.1232)
- Basic exemption: CAD $3,500
- Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YMPE): CAD $71,300
- Maximum annual employee/employer CPP contribution: CAD $4,034.10 each
- Maximum self-employed CPP contribution: CAD $8,068.20
- Maximum CPP2 contribution (employee/employer): CAD $416 each (2026 est.)

**CPP2 2026:** Maximum contribution CAD $416 per side (employee and employer). (CRA 2026)

### Employment Insurance (EI)

- Employee premium rate (2025): 1.64% on insurable earnings (EY p.1233)
- Maximum insurable earnings: CAD $65,700 (2025)
- Maximum annual employee premium: CAD $1,077.48 (2025)
- Employer premium: 1.4 × employee rate = 2.296% (up to max insurable earnings)
- Self-employed: may opt in voluntarily; do not pay EI premiums otherwise

**Quebec:** Quebec residents have lower EI rates (1.31% employee / 1.834% employer) because they also pay into the Quebec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP). (EY p.1233)

| Contribution | Employee | Employer | Self-employed |
|---|---|---|---|
| CPP (base) | 5.95% | 5.95% | 11.90% |
| EI (outside Quebec) | 1.64% | 2.296% | optional/nil |
| **Total (outside Quebec)** | **~7.59%** | **~8.246%** | **~11.90% CPP only** |

**Income ceiling:**
- CPP: YMPE ~$71,300 (2025); CPP2 YAMPE ~$81,200 (2025); indexed annually
- EI: maximum insurable earnings CAD $65,700 (2025)

**Notes:**
- CPP contributions are eligible for a federal tax credit (15% × contribution, refundable mechanism via T1)
- EI premiums are eligible for a federal tax credit (15% × premiums)
- Self-employed pay both CPP sides (11.9%) but receive a 50% deduction (the "employer half") against income

## Self-Employment Regimes

### Option A — Sole Proprietorship (self-employment income on T1)

- **Who qualifies:** Any individual carrying on business in Canada; no formal registration required (though CRA Business Number recommended if invoicing). Income reported directly on T1 General under "self-employment income."
- **Tax base:** Net business income = revenue minus all reasonable business expenses (rent, equipment, professional fees, home office, travel, vehicle, etc.). Deductible costs are broadly similar to corporation rules. (EY p.1228)
- **Rate / mechanism:**
  1. Net business income flows directly into personal income and is taxed at progressive federal + provincial rates.
  2. No separate corporate tax layer.
  3. RRSP contributions (up to 18% of prior-year earned income, max ~$32,490) can shelter income.
  4. CPP: self-employed pay both sides (11.9%); the employer half (5.95%) is deductible from income.
- **Social security:** Full CPP (11.9%) on net self-employment income up to YMPE (~$71,300); CPP2 on earnings up to YAMPE. No EI unless voluntarily enrolled.
- **VAT obligations:** GST/HST registration required once revenue exceeds CAD $30,000 over any four consecutive quarters. Ontario HST rate: 13% (5% federal GST + 8% Ontario provincial). Must collect, remit, and file returns. Input tax credits available.
- **Key limits:** No income tax deferral (unlike corporations). High combined marginal rates at higher incomes (53.53% in Ontario). No liability protection.

### Option B — Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation (CCPC)

- **Who qualifies:** Self-employed professional or consultant who incorporates. Provides tax deferral benefits.
- **Tax base:** Corporate income taxed at low small business rate first; dividends/salary extracted separately.
- **Rate / mechanism:**
  1. CCPC active business income up to small business deduction limit ($500,000): federal rate 9% + Ontario provincial 3.2% = **12.2% combined** (approximate 2025).
  2. Salary extracted: deductible at corporate level; subject to personal income tax + CPP at personal level.
  3. Dividends extracted: eligible or ineligible; personal tax credit mechanism ensures overall tax ≈ as if earned directly (integration principle).
  4. **Tax deferral advantage:** CCPC pays 12.2% corporate tax now; personal tax on dividends paid later — deferral of up to ~40% tax on retained earnings.
- **Social security:** CPP on salary extracted. If taking dividends only, no CPP.
- **VAT obligations:** Same GST/HST registration requirements as sole proprietor.
- **Key limits:** Professional corporations have restrictions by licensing body. Accounting/legal costs. Passive investment income in CCPC phases out the small business deduction above $50,000/year passive income.

## Special Regimes for Newcomers / Expats

### No Specific Expatriate Preferential Regime

Canada does not offer a general preferential personal income tax regime for newcomers (unlike some European countries). Foreign-source income of new residents is generally taxable in Canada once residency is established.

### Part-Year Residency

- **Eligibility:** Individuals who become Canadian residents during the tax year (e.g., arriving immigrants).
- **Benefit:** Taxed on Canadian-source income for the non-resident period and worldwide income for the resident period. Personal credits are prorated for the period of residency.
- **Duration:** Applies to the year of arrival/departure only.
- **Caveats:** "Departure tax" applies when ceasing Canadian residency — deemed disposition of most assets at fair market value. (EY p.1228)

### TFSA for New Residents

- **Eligibility:** Any Canadian resident aged 18+, including new immigrants (from date of residency).
- **Benefit:** Annual CAD $7,000 contribution room (2024+); investment earnings and withdrawals completely tax-free. Cannot contribute for years prior to becoming a resident.
- **Caveats:** Over-contributions subject to 1%/month penalty tax.

### RRSP

- **Eligibility:** Canadian residents with earned income (employment or self-employment).
- **Benefit:** Contributions deductible from taxable income; deferral until withdrawal (typically at retirement, at lower marginal rates).
- **Key limit:** 18% of prior-year earned income, max $32,490 (2025); unused room carries forward indefinitely.

## 2026 Changes

- **Federal rate cut to 14%:** Effective for the full 2026 tax year (rate was 14.5% for 2025 as it applied only from 1 July 2025). This reduces the effective federal tax on the first $58,523 bracket from 15% → 14%. (CRA 2026)
- **Federal bracket indexation (2%):** All federal thresholds increased by 2.0% for 2026 (e.g., top of first bracket: $57,375 → $58,523; top of second: $114,750 → $117,045, etc.). (CRA T4127 2026)
- **BPA increased to $16,452:** Up from $16,129 in 2025 (2% indexation). (CRA 2026)
- **Ontario bracket indexation (1.9%):** Lower four Ontario brackets indexed; the $150,000 and $220,000 upper thresholds remain fixed.
- **CPP2 maximum 2026:** Maximum CPP2 contribution estimated at CAD $416 per side (employee/employer). (CRA 2026)
- **EI premium rate 2026:** Not confirmed at EY content date; expected to remain ~1.64% (indexed based on EI account balance).
- **Canadian Entrepreneurs' Incentive:** New regime reducing taxable capital gains from qualifying small business shares from 50% to 33.33% inclusion, phasing in from 2025 (CAD $400,000 limit in 2025 growing to CAD $2M by 2029). (EY p.1227)
- **First Home Savings Account (FHSA):** CAD $8,000/year contribution room (up to $40,000 lifetime); tax-deductible contributions, tax-free withdrawals for first home purchases. Available since 2023. (EY p.1229)

## Effective Rate Examples

> Single person, no dependants. Federal + Ontario combined.
> Exchange rate: 1 EUR = CAD 1.47 (2025 average).
> "Employment" = gross T4 salary; employee-side CPP + EI + federal + Ontario income tax shown.
> "B2B Sole Proprietor" = net business income (= revenue, assuming minimal expenses for clean comparison); self-employed CPP (11.9%) + federal + Ontario income tax.
> RRSP contributions not modeled. No Ontario surtax at these income levels except at $100k scenario.

### Working: CAD 44,100 (≈ €30,000) Employment

| Step | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Gross salary | | CAD $44,100 |
| CPP employee (5.95% on $44,100 − $3,500 exemption) | 5.95% × $40,600 | CAD $2,416 |
| EI employee (1.64% × $44,100, max $65,700) | 1.64% × $44,100 | CAD $723 |
| Federal taxable income | $44,100 (before credits) | CAD $44,100 |
| Federal tax (14% × $44,100) | $6,174 less: BPA credit (14% × $16,452 = $2,303), CPP credit (14% × $2,416 = $338), EI credit (14% × $723 = $101) | CAD $6,174 − $2,742 = **$3,432** |
| Ontario tax (5.05% × $44,100) | $2,227 less: Ontario BPA credit (5.05% × $12,440 = $628), CPP credit ($122), EI credit ($37) | $2,227 − $787 = **$1,440** |
| **Total burden** | $2,416 + $723 + $3,432 + $1,440 | **$8,011** |
| **Effective rate** | $8,011 / $44,100 | **18.2%** |

### Working: CAD 44,100 (≈ €30,000) Sole Proprietor

| Step | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Net business income | | CAD $44,100 |
| CPP self-employed (11.9% on $40,600) | 11.9% × $40,600 | CAD $4,831 |
| CPP employer-half deduction (50%) | $4,831 / 2 | CAD $2,416 |
| Federal taxable income after CPP deduction | $44,100 − $2,416 | CAD $41,684 |
| Federal tax | 14% × $41,684 = $5,836 less credits (BPA $2,303 + CPP credit $338) | $5,836 − $2,641 = **$3,195** |
| Ontario tax | 5.05% × $41,684 = $2,105 less Ontario credits (~$750) | **$1,355** |
| **Total burden** | $4,831 + $3,195 + $1,355 | **$9,381** |
| **Effective rate** | $9,381 / $44,100 | **21.3%** |

**CPP + EI for employment:**
- $44,100: CPP $2,416 + EI $723 = $3,139
- $88,200: CPP $4,034 (capped; YMPE $71,300) + EI $1,077 (capped) = $5,111 → approx $5,157 total
- $147,000: CPP capped $4,034 + EI capped $1,077 = $5,111 → approx $5,157

**CPP self-employed ($88,200, $147,000):** Both sides of CPP (11.9%) up to YMPE; capped at $8,068/year.

**Key insights:**
- Employment is cheaper than sole proprietorship at all income levels shown, primarily because the employer pays half of CPP (5.95%) — a cost not visible to the employee but borne by the employer. For self-employed, both sides of CPP come out of the business revenue.
- At CAD $147,000 (≈€100k), the gap narrows because CPP is capped; additional income is only subject to income tax marginal rates, not additional CPP. The top combined Ontario + federal rate for income in the $107,785–$150,000 range is: 26% + 11.16% = ~37.16%.
- CCPC incorporation provides significant tax deferral at higher incomes (e.g., at $147,000, only ~12% corporate tax vs. 26%+ personal rate on retained earnings).

**Assumptions:**
- EUR/CAD = 1.47 (approximate 2025 average)
- Federal BPA $16,452; Ontario BPA ~$12,440 (2026)
- CPP/EI credits claimed at 14% federal / 5.05% Ontario on contributions
- CPP YMPE $71,300 (2025; 2026 figure not yet confirmed at EY content date — estimated similar/indexed)
- EI max insurable $65,700 (2025; indexed)
- No Ontario surtax triggered at these income levels
- No RRSP, TFSA, or other tax-planning measures modeled
- CPP employer half: 5.95% deductible for self-employed (50% of 11.9% total)
