## Personal Income Tax

Three-basket progressive system (impuesto sobre la renta) for residents taxed on worldwide income. Tax year = calendar year. Filing dates between August and October of the following year, staggered by last two digits of NIT. Three income baskets: (1) General basket (employment, non-employment, capital income); (2) Pensions; (3) Dividends. (EY p.284–285)

**Key reference unit: UVT (Unidad de Valor Tributario)**
- UVT 2025 = COP 49,799 (confirmed by EY p.285)
- UVT 2026 = COP 52,374 (DIAN Resolution 000238, 15 December 2025; +5.17% increase)

**Monthly withholding tax rates on employment income — 2025** (EY p.287)

| Monthly income — Exceeding (UVT) | Not exceeding (UVT) | Marginal rate (%) | Tax calculation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 95 | 0 | 0 |
| 95 | 150 | 19 | (income in UVT − 95) × 19% |
| 150 | 360 | 28 | (income in UVT − 150) × 28% + UVT 10 |
| 360 | 640 | 33 | (income in UVT − 360) × 33% + UVT 69 |
| 640 | 945 | 35 | (income in UVT − 640) × 35% + UVT 162 |
| 945 | 2,300 | 37 | (income in UVT − 945) × 37% + UVT 268 |
| 2,300 | — | 39 | (income in UVT − 2,300) × 39% + UVT 770 |

Note: Withholding base = 75% of gross employment income (after mandatory SS contributions and the 25% exempt income deduction). The 25% exemption on employment income is limited to UVT 790/year; total deductions/exemptions capped at 40% of gross income less mandatory SS, and cannot exceed UVT 1,340/year (≈ COP 66,731,000 for 2025; ≈ COP 70,181,160 for 2026). (EY p.285–286)

|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 1,090 | 0 | 0 |
| 1,090 | 1,700 | 19 | (income in UVT − 1,090) × 19% |
| 1,700 | 4,100 | 28 | (income in UVT − 1,700) × 28% + UVT 116 |
| 4,100 | 8,670 | 33 | (income in UVT − 4,100) × 33% + UVT 788 |
| 8,670 | 18,970 | 35 | (income in UVT − 8,670) × 35% + UVT 2,296 |
| 18,970 | 31,000 | 37 | (income in UVT − 18,970) × 37% + UVT 5,901 |
| 31,000 | — | 39 | (income in UVT − 31,000) × 39% + UVT 10,352 |

**Notes:**
- Nonresident annual rate: flat 35% on Colombian-source income (EY p.288)
- Capital gains (assets held > 2 years, inheritances): flat 15%, taxed separately from ordinary income (EY p.285, 289)
- Dividends from profits already taxed at corporate level: progressive 0%–39% for residents; flat 20% for nonresidents (EY p.288)
- Dividends from untaxed profits: flat 35% corporate equivalent + dividend tax (EY p.288)
- Pensions: annual pension income in the general basket; annual exemption up to UVT 12,000 (~COP 628 million for 2026); foreign pension may also be exempt since January 2023 depending on nature (EY p.285)

**Standard deductions / allowances for employment income:** (EY p.285–286)
- Mandatory SS contributions (health and pension): reduce taxable income
- 25% of employment income exempt (limit: UVT 790/year ≈ COP 41,375,460 for 2026)
- Economic dependants deduction: UVT 72 per dependant per year (up to 4 dependants) — separate from the UVT 1,340 cap (from January 2023) (EY p.286)
- 1% deduction for payments made with electronic invoices (capped at UVT 240/year) — separate from the UVT 1,340 cap
- Medical/health plan payments to private health enterprises
- Interest on loans for acquisition of main home
- ICETEX educational loans: up to UVT 100/year (≈ COP 5,237,400 for 2026)
- AFC housing savings account contributions and voluntary pension contributions (exempt up to AFC + voluntary pension ≤ 30% of gross income)
- Total general deductions/exemptions (excluding dependants and 1% electronic invoice): capped at 40% of gross income minus mandatory SS, and not exceeding UVT 1,340/year

**Taxable income reductions for self-employment income:** (EY p.285)
- Business expenses OR exempt income of 25% (with applicable limitations)
- Mandatory health and pension contributions deductible
- Deductions/exemptions capped at 40% of gross income less mandatory SS; annual cap UVT 1,340

## Social Security

Employers and employees contribute to mandatory health and pension systems. Base salary contribution is capped at 25 × MMLS (Minimum Monthly Legal Salary) per month. MMLS for 2025 = COP 1,423,500 (referenced in EY p.290); 2026 MMLS updated annually by government decree (typically January). (EY p.289–290)

| Contribution | Employer (%) | Employee (%) |
|---|---|---|
| Health (Salud) | 8.5 | 4.0 |
| Pensions (Pensiones) | 12.0 | 4.0 |
| Additional pension solidarity fund (a) | — | 1–2 (see note) |
| Labor risks (Riesgos Laborales — ARL) (b) | 0.522–8.7 | — |
| **Standard totals (without ARL variable)** | **20.5%** | **8.0%** |

(a) Additional 1% pension solidarity fund contribution applies to employees earning ≥ 4 MMLS/month (≥ COP 5,694,000 for 2025). Progressive additional contributions being reviewed by Constitutional Court (pension reform):
- 4–7 MMLS: +1.5%
- 7–11 MMLS: +1.8%
- 11–15 MMLS: +2.5%
- 15–19 MMLS: +2.8%
- ≥ 20 MMLS: +3.0%
(EY p.289; reform under constitutional review as of October 2025)

(b) Labor risk (ARL) contribution: 0.522%–8.7% of salary; paid entirely by employer; variable by activity type. (EY p.290)

**Employer payroll taxes (parafiscales):** Employers also pay 9% of salary (no cap) distributed as: Family Compensation Funds (Cajas de Compensación) 4% + ICBF 3% + SENA 2%. Companies in free trade zones with certain conditions: exempt from ICBF/SENA for employees earning < 10 MMLS. (EY p.290)

**SBC cap:** 25 MMLS/month (≈ COP 35,587,500/month for 2025; COP 35.6M+ for 2026). (EY p.290)

**Note on foreign employees:** Under Law 1819 of 2016 and Law 797 of 2003, foreign employees are considered voluntary affiliates to the pension system if covered by a pension plan abroad and paying contributions for disability, retirement, and death risk. They may be exempt from mandatory pension contributions with a certificate from the foreign pension entity. Mandatory health contributions remain (8.5% employer + 4% employee). (EY p.289)

**Self-employed contributions:** Independent workers must contribute to mandatory health (12.5% total on 40% of gross fees — statutory base, with minimum base floor) and pension (16% total on same base). Since 2023, the contribution base for independents is 40% of monthly fees, with health at 12.5% and pension at 16% on that base. (derived from standard DIAN/UGPP rules; EY does not detail this explicitly)

## Self-Employment Regimes

### Option A — Régimen Simple de Tributación (SIMPLE)

- **Who qualifies:** Resident individuals (personas naturales) carrying out business or professional activities; also certain companies. Annual gross income ≤ 100,000 UVT (≈ COP 5,237,400,000 / ~€1.2M for 2026) — general activities. For professional services (liberal professions — actividades donde predomina el factor intelectual): ceiling is 12,000 UVT (≈ COP 628,488,000 / ~€146k for 2026). Must have no tax debts, be registered in the RUT, and not be a financial entity, foreign company, or similar excluded entity. (Art. 905–909 Estatuto Tributario; DIAN micrositio RST)
- **Tax base:** Gross income actually collected in the period — no expense deductions.
- **Rate / mechanism:** Single consolidated rate covers income tax + ICA (industry and commerce tax) + other applicable taxes. Rates for Category 3 — Professional, consulting, and scientific services (where intellectual factor predominates), confirmed for 2026 (DIAN micrositio tarifas-rst):

  | Annual gross income (UVT) | Annual gross income (COP, UVT 2026 = 52,374) | Rate (%) |
  |---|---|---|
  | 0 – 6,000 | 0 – 314,244,000 | 5.9 |
  | 6,000 – 15,000 | 314,244,000 – 785,610,000 | 7.3 |
  | 15,000 – 30,000 | 785,610,000 – 1,571,220,000 | 12.0 |
  | 30,000 – 100,000 | 1,571,220,000 – 5,237,400,000 | 14.5 |

  Note: The separate "category 4" designation was removed by the Constitutional Court (Sentencia C-540 of 2023), which struck down the Ley 2277 of 2022 subdivisions. Professional services are now Category 3 under the original structure. Ceiling for liberal professions within SIMPLE: 12,000 UVT/year. (DIAN, actualicese.com)

- **Social security:** Independent workers in SIMPLE must still pay mandatory health and pension contributions separately (as self-employed). Health: 12.5% on 40% of monthly gross fees. Pension: 16% on 40% of monthly gross fees. Minimum base: 1 MMLS. These are NOT included in the SIMPLE rate.
- **VAT obligations:** SIMPLE taxpayers are not subject to IVA collection/remittance if they do not carry out VAT-taxable activities; professional services (honorarios) may be VAT-exempt under Colombian law depending on the service.
- **Key limits:** 12,000 UVT ceiling for liberal professions (~€146k at 1 EUR ≈ COP 4,300); cannot have income from employment in same year (if simultaneously employed); no retención en la fuente applies to SIMPLE-registered taxpayers (must show RUT with code 47 to clients). Payments made bimestrally as advance payments.

### Option B — Régimen Ordinario (Standard Regime — Actividad Independiente)

- **Who qualifies:** All resident individuals with self-employment or business income; no income ceiling.
- **Tax base:** Gross income minus deductible expenses minus mandatory SS contributions minus applicable exemptions. Business expenses: actual documented expenses OR 25% exempt income deduction (with limitations). Total deductions capped at 40% of gross income less SS, max UVT 1,340. (EY p.285)
- **Rate / mechanism:** Same progressive annual rate scale as employment income (0%–39%); self-employed are subject to retención en la fuente (withholding) by clients at applicable rates (typically 10%–11% for professional services).
- **Social security:** Mandatory health and pension (self-employed rates: health 12.5%, pension 16%, each applied to 40% of gross monthly fees; minimum base = 1 MMLS).
- **Key limits:** No income ceiling; full expense deductibility can reduce taxable income substantially for high-expense activities. Better than SIMPLE when expenses are significant or income exceeds SIMPLE category ceilings.

## Special Regimes for Newcomers / Expats

### No General Preferential Expat Regime

Colombia does not have a formal tax incentive specifically for new residents (no "lump sum" or flat-rate option). All residents are taxed on worldwide income under the standard progressive scale. (EY p.284)

### Nonresident Short-Stay Rule

- **Eligibility:** Individuals present in Colombia for fewer than 183 continuous or discontinuous days in any consecutive 365-day period.
- **Benefit:** Taxed as nonresident on Colombian-source income only; flat 35% rate (instead of progressive 0%–39% on worldwide income). (EY p.284, 288)
- **Caveat:** Once 183-day threshold is met, full resident taxation applies for the entire year.

### Wealth Tax (Impuesto al Patrimonio)

- Residents with net worth ≥ UVT 72,000 as of 1 January 2026 (~COP 3,770,928,000 / ~€877k) are required to file a wealth tax return for the 2026 tax year. Progressive rates 0%–1.5% on net equity. The 1.5% rate applies only for 2023–2026. (EY p.291)
- Nonresidents: same obligation but only for assets located in Colombia.

### Digital Nomad Visa

Colombia issues a "Digital Nomad" Visitor Visa subcategory (V-type), valid up to 2 years. Foreign income earned remotely does not constitute Colombian-source income, so short-stay nomads (< 183 days) avoid Colombian income tax. (EY p.294)

## 2026 Changes

- **UVT 2026 = COP 52,374** (DIAN Resolution 000238, 15 December 2025; +5.17% vs. 2025 UVT of COP 49,799). All bracket thresholds, deduction ceilings, and contribution bases expressed in UVT update automatically.
- **SIMPLE rates 2026:** Unchanged from 2025 (5.9% → 14.5% for professional services). Category 3 ceiling for liberal professions: 12,000 UVT = COP 628,488,000 for 2026.
- **Pension reform under review:** The progressive additional solidarity fund contributions (1.5%–3.0%) from Ley 2277 of 2022 are under Constitutional Court review. If approved, employees earning ≥ 4 MMLS/month would face additional pension contributions. (EY p.289)
- **Wealth tax 1.5% rate continues through 2026:** The 1.5% top rate on net equity applies for 2026 as the final year of this exceptional rate level. (EY p.291)
- **Filing thresholds in UVT terms for 2025 tax year:** Annual gross income ≥ UVT 1,400 (≈ COP 69,719,000) triggers mandatory filing; gross assets or equity ≥ UVT 4,500 (≈ COP 224,096,000). (EY p.291)

## Effective Rate Examples

> Single person, no dependants, no personal deductions beyond mandatory SS (simplified baseline).
> EUR/COP rate: 1 EUR = COP 4,300 (approximate 2025 average).
> UVT 2026 = COP 52,374.
> "Employment" = gross salary; employee-side taxes (income tax + employee SS 8% base + solidarity fund not applicable at these levels).
> "SIMPLE B2B" = gross revenue; SIMPLE rate on gross + mandatory self-employed SS (health 12.5% + pension 16% on 40% of gross fees).
> The 25% employment income exemption (capped at UVT 790) applied for employment calculations.

### Definitions for calculations
- 1 EUR ≈ COP 4,300
- €30,000 = COP 129,000,000
- €60,000 = COP 258,000,000
- €100,000 = COP 430,000,000
- UVT 2026 = COP 52,374

Convert COP to UVT: divide by 52,374.

**Key insight — SIMPLE crossover:** At ~€30k the SIMPLE rate (17.3%) is slightly worse than employment (12.3%) because employee SS is only 8% vs. self-employed SS on 40% of gross (≈11.4% effective). At €60k and above, SIMPLE becomes more favorable (17.3% vs. 22%) as the progressive income tax advantage outweighs the higher SS base. At €100k SIMPLE is clearly better (18.7% vs. 28.3%).

Note: SIMPLE SS contribution is higher in absolute terms because self-employed pay both employee and "employer-equivalent" health/pension contributions (total 28.5% on 40% of gross = 11.4% effective on gross), vs. employee side only (8% directly). But the SIMPLE income tax (5.9%–7.3% on gross) is far lower than employment progressive PIT.

**Assumptions:**
- EUR/COP = 4,300 (approximate 2025 average; COP has been volatile)
- UVT 2026 = COP 52,374
- Employee SS: 4% health + 4% pension = 8% (solidarity fund not applied: income < 4 MMLS threshold at €30k; above 4 MMLS at €60k/€100k but pension reform not yet enacted — not included)
- Self-employed SS: 12.5% health + 16% pension = 28.5% on 40% of monthly gross fees
- 25% employment income exemption applied up to UVT 790 cap (= COP 41,375,460 for 2026)
- No other personal deductions claimed
- ARL (labor risk) not included — employer cost for employees; no equivalent for self-employed in standard SIMPLE
- Parafiscales (SENA/ICBF/Cajas) are employer costs, not shown
- Capital gains and dividend income not modeled

## Known Gaps

- [ ] MMLS for 2026 not confirmed in EY or search results (announced December/January each year); calculations use 2025 MMLS = COP 1,423,500 for SS cap reference; 2026 MMLS will be higher
- [ ] Self-employed SS calculation (28.5% on 40% of gross) uses the statutory rule from UGPP; EY does not explicitly detail independent worker SS rates; verify at ugpp.gov.co
- [ ] Pension reform (additional solidarity contributions 1.5%–3%) pending Constitutional Court ruling; if enacted, employee SS at higher incomes would increase by 1.5–3 percentage points
- [ ] SIMPLE rate crossover between standard employment and SIMPLE changes if pension reform passes (would make employment more expensive, improving SIMPLE's relative attractiveness)
- [ ] ICA (industry and commerce tax) included in SIMPLE rate but not modeled separately; municipalities set ICA rates (typically 0.2%–1.4%); in the standard regime, ICA is an additional local tax
- [ ] Retención en la fuente rates for independent workers under standard regime not computed (typically 10%–11% for professional services — provisional, credited against annual tax)
- [ ] Voluntary AFC contributions and voluntary pension deductions (powerful tools for high earners in standard regime) not modeled
- [ ] Wealth tax (0%–1.5% on net equity ≥ UVT 72,000 ≈ COP 3.77 billion) not included — only relevant at very high asset levels (~€877k+)
