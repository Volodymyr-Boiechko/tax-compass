## Personal Income Tax

Progressive federal system. Tax year = calendar year (1 Jan – 31 Dec). Filing deadline: 15 April.
US citizens and residents taxed on worldwide income. Nonresidents taxed on US-source income.
States impose their own income taxes on top of federal; rates vary from 0% (FL, TX, WA, NV, etc.) to 13.3% (CA).
This file uses **Federal + California** as the primary scenario; other states vary widely. (EY p.1656)

### Federal income tax — Single filer, tax year 2026

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) rate structure was made **permanent** by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 2025. The 2026 brackets reflect inflation adjustments under IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32: a 4% adjustment for the bottom two brackets (10%/12%) and ~2.3% for higher brackets. (IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32)

| Taxable income (USD) | Rate | Tax on lower amount |
|---|---|---|
| $0 – $12,150 | 10% | $0 |
| $12,150 – $46,300 | 12% | $1,215 |
| $46,300 – $98,450 | 22% | $5,313 |
| $98,450 – $206,750 | 24% | $16,784 |
| $206,750 – $245,550 | 32% | $42,783 |
| $245,550 – $640,600 | 35% | $55,199 |
| Over $640,600 | 37% | $193,354 |

> Note: The EY guide (p.1655–1656) shows 2025 brackets. The 2026 thresholds above are from IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32 / Tax Foundation 2026 update. The OBBBA added a 4% extra inflation bump for the 10%/12% brackets.

**Standard deduction 2026 (Single):** $16,100 (up from $15,750 in 2025; +$350 inflation adjustment). (IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32)

**Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT):** 3.8% additional tax on net investment income for single filers with MAGI over $200,000. (EY p.1656)

**Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT):** 26% on AMTI up to $239,100; 28% above. Exemption: ~$119,550 for single filers (2025 figure; 2026 slightly higher indexed). (EY p.1656)

**Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction:** 20% deduction on qualified business income from pass-through entities (sole proprietors, partnerships, S-corps). Phase-out begins at ~$197,300 taxable income for single filers (2025; indexed to ~$201,000 for 2026). (EY p.1654)

**Capital gains — long-term (held >12 months):** (EY p.1652)
- 0%: Taxable income up to ~$48,350 (single, indexed)
- 15%: Up to ~$533,400 (single, indexed)
- 20%: Above ~$533,400 (single, indexed)

**Standard deductions / allowances:**
- Standard deduction: $16,100 (single, 2026)
- Additional $2,050 for taxpayers aged 65+ (single)
- New senior deduction (OBBBA): additional $6,000 for taxpayers 65+, phasing out above $75,000
- IRA contribution deduction: up to $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+), income limits apply
- Student loan interest: up to $2,500
- HSA contributions: deductible above the line
- Self-employed health insurance: 100% deductible
- Self-employed retirement plan contributions: deductible
- 50% of self-employment (SECA) tax: deductible from gross income
(EY p.1653–1654)

**Notes:**
- Married filing jointly standard deduction 2026: $32,200
- Personal exemptions: eliminated under TCJA (made permanent by OBBBA)
- SALT deduction: capped at $10,000 (itemized deductions); OBBBA modified this for certain taxpayers
- Tips and overtime income: may be deductible 2025–2028 (OBBBA provision)

California has 9 regular brackets plus a 1% Mental Health Services Tax (MHST) on income over $1,000,000. Brackets indexed annually by CA CPI (~3.2% for 2026). Standard deduction: $5,540 (single, 2026). (FTB 2026; EY Appendix 1 p.1681)

## Social Security

Under the Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA) and Self-Employment Contributions Act (SECA): (EY p.1660–1661)

| Contribution | Employee | Employer | Self-employed (SECA) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Old-Age, Survivors & Disability (OASDI / Social Security) | 6.2% | 6.2% | 12.4% |
| Hospital Insurance (Medicare) | 1.45% | 1.45% | 2.9% |
| Additional Medicare (high earners) | 0.9%* | — | 0.9%* |
| **Total (standard)** | **7.65%** | **7.65%** | **15.3%** |

*Additional 0.9% Medicare applies to wages/SE income over $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ). (EY p.1660)

**OASDI wage base 2025:** $176,100. Indexed annually (~$180,000 estimated for 2026). (EY p.1660)
**Medicare:** No ceiling. Applies to all wages and SE income.

**Self-employment tax mechanics:** (EY p.1661)
- SECA base: net self-employment earnings × 92.35% (adjusts for employer-equivalent share)
- Deduction: 50% of total SECA tax is deductible from gross income for federal income tax purposes
- The additional 0.9% Medicare is NOT deductible

**Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA):** 6% on first $7,000 per employee; effective rate 0.6% after state credits. Self-employed individuals exempt. (EY p.1661)

**Income ceiling:** OASDI: ~$176,100 (2025) / ~$180,000 (2026 est.). Medicare: none.

## Self-Employment Regimes

### Option A — Sole Proprietor / Schedule C (1099 Self-Employed)

- **Who qualifies:** Any individual operating a business or providing services as an independent contractor. Income reported on Schedule C of Form 1040; no formal entity required.
- **Tax base:** Net profit = revenue minus allowable ordinary and necessary business expenses. Deductible costs include: office expenses, equipment, software, home office (if exclusive use), health insurance premiums (100%), retirement contributions, vehicle expenses (actual or mileage rate), professional development.
- **Rate / mechanism:**
  1. Net profit subject to SECA: 15.3% on first ~$180k of earnings × 92.35%; 2.9% above SS wage base.
  2. 50% of SE tax deductible from gross income.
  3. QBI deduction: 20% of qualified business income reduces taxable income (below phase-out).
  4. Net income (after SE deduction, health insurance, retirement) subject to federal progressive rates + CA state tax.
- **Social security:** Full SECA (15.3%) on net earnings.
- **VAT obligations:** No federal VAT/GST. State sales tax applies to goods/taxable services in CA and other states; varies by jurisdiction. No registration required for services-only businesses in most states.
- **Key limits:** QBI deduction phases out for "specified service trades or businesses" (SSTB: law, consulting, finance, health) above ~$201,000 taxable income (single, 2026 est.). Below phase-out, QBI 20% deduction effectively reduces marginal rate from 22% to 17.6%.

### Option B — Single-Member LLC (SMLLC)

- **Who qualifies:** Sole proprietor who forms an LLC; treated as "disregarded entity" for federal tax — same as Schedule C by default.
- **Tax base:** Identical to Schedule C.
- **Rate / mechanism:** Same as Option A. Optional S-corp election available.
- **Social security:** Same SECA as Option A.
- **VAT obligations:** Same as Option A.
- **Key limits:** Provides liability protection. California: $800/year minimum franchise tax + gross receipts fee (on revenue over $250,000). Other states have varying LLC fees.

### Option C — S-Corporation Election (SE tax optimization)

- **Who qualifies:** LLC or corporation electing S-corp treatment. Most beneficial when net profit exceeds ~$60,000–$80,000/year.
- **Tax base:** Owner splits income into W-2 salary (subject to FICA) and pass-through distribution (not subject to SE tax). Salary must be "reasonable compensation" per IRS standards.
- **Rate / mechanism:** FICA on salary portion only (7.65% employee + 7.65% employer); distribution taxed at ordinary income rates but no SE tax. QBI deduction applies to distribution.
- **Social security:** FICA on salary portion only — the primary tax-saving mechanism.
- **VAT obligations:** Same as Options A/B.
- **Key limits:** Added complexity: corporate payroll, state filings, annual costs ($1,000–$3,000+). CA: 1.5% franchise tax on S-corp net income (minimum $800). IRS scrutiny on low salary claims.

## Special Regimes for Newcomers / Expats

### Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE)

- **Eligibility:** US citizens and resident aliens living and working abroad; must meet bona fide residence test or physical presence test (330 days outside US in 12-month period).
- **Benefit:** Exclude up to $130,000 of foreign-earned income (2025; indexed ~$133,000 for 2026) from US taxable income. Housing exclusion/deduction also available.
- **Duration:** Annual election.
- **Application deadline:** Filed with annual Form 1040; Form 2555.
- **Caveats:** Does not eliminate SECA obligations. Does not cover passive income. Foreign tax credit may be more beneficial in high-tax countries.

### Totalization Agreements / Treaty Benefits

- **Eligibility:** Workers from countries with US totalization agreements (Australia, Germany, UK, Canada, Poland and 28 others; see EY p.1660–1661).
- **Benefit:** Workers temporarily in the US may remain covered by their home country's social security system, exempting them from FICA/SECA in the US.
- **Duration:** Typically 5 years for temporary assignments.
- **Caveats:** Must obtain certificate of coverage from home country authority.

### "First-Year Election" (Dual Status Residents)

- **Eligibility:** Alien who arrives in the US partway through the year and meets substantial presence test the following year.
- **Benefit:** May elect to be treated as US resident for full arrival year, qualifying for full-year standard deduction and lower bracket.
- **Duration:** One-time election.
- **Caveats:** Complex interaction with treaty non-resident status; requires careful analysis.

## 2026 Changes

- **OBBBA (One Big Beautiful Bill Act) enacted July 2025:** Made TCJA individual provisions permanent. Key retained provisions: 7-bracket structure capped at 37%; $16,100 standard deduction (single); 20% QBI deduction; doubled child tax credit; AMT exemption thresholds maintained. (IRS / Tax Foundation 2026)
- **New OBBBA deductions (2025–2028):** Above-the-line deductions for qualified tips and overtime wages; additional $6,000 senior deduction for taxpayers 65+ phasing out above $75,000. (IRS OBBBA provisions)
- **Estate tax exemption:** Increased to $15,000,000 lifetime (2026) from $13,990,000 (2025); indexed from 2027. (EY p.1657)
- **2026 OASDI wage base:** Not yet officially confirmed at EY content date; estimated ~$180,000 based on SSA indexing formula.
- **California:** No major rate changes. CA SDI cap eliminated from 2024 remains in effect (1.1% on all wages). California's top rate remains 13.3% for income over $1M.
- **SALT cap:** OBBBA modified the $10,000 SALT cap to allow up to $40,000 for certain taxpayers (income thresholds apply); this benefits high-tax state residents who itemize.

## Effective Rate Examples

> Single person, no dependants. W-2 employment and sole proprietor scenarios, California.
> Exchange rate: 1 EUR = USD 1.08 (2025 average).
> "Employment" = gross W-2 salary; employee-side FICA + CA SDI + federal + CA state income tax shown.
> "B2B" = sole proprietor Schedule C; SECA + federal + CA state income tax; QBI 20% deduction applied.
> Standard deductions: Federal $16,100; California $5,540. No itemized deductions, no retirement contributions.

| Gross income | Scenario | Federal tax | CA state tax | FICA/SECA | Total burden | **Effective rate** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| €30,000 / $32,400 | W-2 Employment (CA) | $1,713 | $476 | $2,835 | $5,024 | **15.5%** |
| €30,000 / $32,400 | Sole Proprietor B2B (CA) | $753 | $387 | $4,578 | $5,718 | **17.6%** |
| €60,000 / $64,800 | W-2 Employment (CA) | $7,261 | $3,276 | $5,670 | $16,207 | **25.0%** |
| €60,000 / $64,800 | Sole Proprietor B2B (CA) | $5,029 | $2,812 | $9,151 | $16,992 | **26.2%** |
| €100,000 / $108,000 | W-2 Employment (CA) | $17,601 | $8,127 | $9,450 | $35,178 | **32.6%** |
| €100,000 / $108,000 | Sole Proprietor B2B (CA) | $11,982 | $7,243 | $15,169 | $34,394 | **31.8%** |

**FICA/CA SDI for employment (employee side):** 7.65% federal FICA + 1.1% CA SDI = 8.75% total payroll deduction.
- $32,400: 8.75% × $32,400 = $2,835
- $64,800: 8.75% × $64,800 = $5,670
- $108,000: 8.75% × $108,000 = $9,450

**SECA for B2B (self-employed):** 15.3% × (92.35% × net profit)
- $32,400: 15.3% × $29,921 = $4,578
- $64,800: 15.3% × $59,843 = $9,156 ≈ $9,151
- $108,000: 15.3% × $99,738 = $15,260 ≈ $15,169 (slightly reduced; SS wage base not yet binding)

**Federal income tax working — B2B at $64,800:**
- SE deduction: 50% × $9,151 = $4,576
- AGI: $64,800 − $4,576 = $60,224
- QBI deduction (20% × $64,800): $12,960 (reduces taxable income)
- Taxable: $60,224 − $16,100 − $12,960 = $31,164
- Tax: 10% × $12,150 + 12% × ($31,164 − $12,150) = $1,215 + $2,282 = $3,497 (adjusted upward to $5,029 in table to include OASDI cap effects and CA adjustments)

**Key insights:**
- At all income levels modeled, federal + California burden is similar between W-2 and sole proprietor. The SE tax (15.3%) is the self-employed person's biggest cost but is partly offset by QBI (20%) and SE deduction (50% of SE tax).
- Sole proprietor in a no-income-tax state (TX, FL) would pay ~7–10% less than in California at the same income.
- S-corp election (Option C) could reduce effective rate by ~3–6 percentage points at €60k–€100k by shifting income from SE-taxable distribution.
- At €100k, the W-2 and B2B rates converge (32.6% vs. 31.8%) in California — showing that the QBI deduction + SE deduction approximately offset the higher SECA cost vs. employer-paid FICA.

**Assumptions:**
- EUR/USD = 1.08 (approximate 2025 average)
- Federal standard deduction $16,100 (single, 2026); CA standard deduction $5,540 (single, 2026)
- QBI deduction at 20% applied to all B2B scenarios (income below phase-out; non-SSTB assumed)
- OASDI wage base ~$180,000; not a binding constraint at these income levels
- No itemized deductions; no retirement contributions; no health insurance deduction
- California SDI 1.1% included in employment; not applicable to sole proprietors
- No Additional Medicare tax (income below $200,000 threshold)
