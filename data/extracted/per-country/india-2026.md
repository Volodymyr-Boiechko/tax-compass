## Personal Income Tax

India operates a dual-regime personal income tax system: a **Normal Tax Regime** and a **Concessional Tax Regime** (default from FY 2025-26 / AY 2026-27, i.e., tax year beginning 1 April 2025). Tax year runs from 1 April to 31 March. Residents are taxed on worldwide income; non-residents on India-source income only. (EY pp. 601–618)

### Normal Tax Regime (Old Regime — opt-in from FY 2025-26)

Available to salaried individuals each year and to self-employed individuals (once opted in, irrevocable in subsequent years except under specified conditions). Allows most deductions (80C, 80D, HRA, etc.). (EY p. 615)

| Taxable income (INR) | Tax rate | Tax due (INR) | Cumulative tax (INR) |
|---|---|---|---|
| First 250,000 | 0% | — | 0 |
| Next 250,000 (250,001–500,000) | 5% | 12,500 | 12,500 |
| Next 500,000 (500,001–1,000,000) | 20% | 100,000 | 112,500 |
| Above 1,000,000 | 30% | — | — |

**Surcharge on income tax (Normal Regime):**

| Total taxable income (INR) | Surcharge on tax |
|---|---|
| Up to 5,000,000 | Nil |
| 5,000,001 – 10,000,000 | 10% |
| 10,000,001 – 20,000,000 | 15% |
| 20,000,001 – 50,000,000 | 25% |
| Above 50,000,000 | 37% |

**Health and Education Cess:** 4% on (income tax + surcharge). (EY p. 616)

**Effective maximum marginal rates (Normal Regime):**
- Income ≤ INR 5M: 31.2% (30% + 4% cess)
- Income INR 5M–10M: 34.32% (30% + 10% surcharge + 4% cess)
- Income INR 10M–20M: 35.88%
- Income INR 20M–50M: 39% (25% surcharge)
- Income >INR 50M: 42.744% (37% surcharge)

**Tax rebate (Normal Regime):** Resident individuals with total taxable income up to INR 500,000 receive a rebate equal to the lesser of total tax payable or INR 12,500 — effectively 0% up to INR 500,000. (EY p. 616)

### Concessional Tax Regime (New Regime — DEFAULT from FY 2025-26)

Effective from tax year beginning 1 April 2025 (FY 2025-26), this is the **default regime**. Lower rates but most deductions/exemptions not available. Standard deduction of INR 75,000 available for salaried. (EY pp. 617–618)

| Taxable income (INR) | Tax rate | Tax due (INR) | Cumulative tax (INR) |
|---|---|---|---|
| First 400,000 | 0% | — | 0 |
| Next 400,000 (400,001–800,000) | 5% | 20,000 | 20,000 |
| Next 400,000 (800,001–1,200,000) | 10% | 40,000 | 60,000 |
| Next 400,000 (1,200,001–1,600,000) | 15% | 60,000 | 120,000 |
| Next 400,000 (1,600,001–2,000,000) | 20% | 80,000 | 200,000 |
| Next 400,000 (2,000,001–2,400,000) | 25% | 100,000 | 300,000 |
| Above 2,400,000 | 30% | — | — |

**Surcharge:** Same as Normal Regime, except above INR 50M surcharge capped at 25% (not 37%). (EY p. 618)

**Tax rebate (Concessional Regime):** Resident individuals with total taxable income up to INR 1,200,000 receive a rebate equal to total tax payable or INR 60,000, whichever is less — effectively 0% tax up to INR 1,200,000. (EY p. 618)

### Special Tax Rates

| Income type | Rate |
|---|---|
| Long-term capital gains on listed equity shares / equity MF units (STT paid), above INR 125,000 | 12.5% + surcharge + 4% cess |
| Short-term capital gains on listed equity shares (STT paid) | 20% + surcharge + 4% cess |
| Dividends — resident withholding | 10% (not final; grossed up in return) |
| Virtual digital assets (crypto) | 30% + surcharge + cess; no deductions except cost of acquisition |
| Long-term capital gains on other assets | 12.5% without inflation adjustment (assets transferred on/after 23 July 2024) |

**Standard deduction for employment income (Concessional Regime):** INR 75,000. (EY p. 606)

## Social Security

India's social security is governed by the **Employees' Provident Fund (EPF) Act**. Applies to establishments with 20+ employees. (EY pp. 620–621)

| Contribution | Employee | Employer | Self-employed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Provident Fund (EPF) | 12% of basic pay | 12% of basic pay | Not applicable |
| Employees' Pension Scheme (EPS — within employer 12%) | — | 8.33% | Not applicable |
| Employees' Deposit-Linked Insurance | — | Included above | Not applicable |
| **Total** | **12%** | **12%** | **N/A** |

**Notes:**
- Contributions calculated on "monthly pay" (basic pay + dearness allowance)
- Employer's 12%: 8.33% allocated to EPS, balance to EPF; for employees joining on/after 1 September 2014 with pay > INR 15,000/month, entire employer contribution goes to EPF
- Employer contributions exempt from tax up to 12% of monthly pay; excess taxable (EY p. 605)
- Aggregate employer contributions to PF + NPS + superannuation exceeding INR 750,000/year is taxable in employee's hands (effective 1 April 2020)
- **No mandatory social security for self-employed** — voluntary NPS contributions deductible up to INR 50,000 (Section 80CCD(1B)) under Normal Regime only
- No published income ceiling for EPF; however contributions are based on actual pay up to the cap of the covered establishment

## Self-Employment Regimes

### Option A — Standard Self-Employment with Books (Normal or Concessional Regime)

- **Who qualifies:** All self-employed individuals, freelancers, professionals; required if gross receipts > INR 5,000,000 (professionals) or turnover > INR 30,000,000 (businesses)
- **Tax base:** Net profit after documented business expenses (cash/accrual basis)
- **Rate / mechanism:** Normal Regime (0/5/20/30%) or Concessional Regime (0/5/10/15/20/25/30%) applied to net profit
- **Social security:** Voluntary NPS only; no mandatory EPF
- **VAT/GST obligations:** GST registration mandatory if turnover > INR 2,000,000 (services); standard rate 18% for most professional services
- **Key limits:** Tax audit required if gross receipts > INR 5,000,000 (professionals); filing deadline 31 July or 31 October (if audit required)

### Option B — Presumptive Taxation Section 44ADA (Professionals)

- **Who qualifies:** Specified professionals (legal, medical, engineering, accounting, architecture, technical consulting, IT, film artists) with gross receipts ≤ INR 5,000,000
- **Tax base:** Presumptive income = 50% of gross receipts — no books required, no expense documentation needed
- **Rate / mechanism:** Normal or Concessional Regime rates applied to the 50% presumptive base; effectively halves taxable income vs. gross
- **Social security:** Voluntary only
- **VAT obligations:** GST applies if turnover > INR 2,000,000
- **Key limits:** Gross receipts ≤ INR 5,000,000; if opted, must maintain for 5 consecutive years; not available for non-resident professionals or those with foreign income

### Option C — Presumptive Taxation Section 44AD (Small Business)

- **Who qualifies:** Small businesses (not professionals) with turnover ≤ INR 30,000,000
- **Tax base:** Presumptive income = 8% of gross turnover (6% for digital/banking receipts)
- **Rate / mechanism:** Normal or Concessional Regime rates applied to presumptive base
- **Key limits:** Turnover ≤ INR 30,000,000; if opted, must continue for 5 years

## Special Regimes for Newcomers / Expats

### Not Ordinarily Resident (NOR) Status

- **Eligibility:** Individuals who meet the basic residency test (182 days in India) but were NRI in 9 of the preceding 10 years, or were present in India ≤ 729 days in the previous 7 years (EY pp. 601–602)
- **Benefit:** Taxed only on India-source income and income received/accrued in India; foreign-source income (e.g., foreign employment, foreign bank interest) exempt from Indian tax
- **Duration:** Up to approximately 3 years after returning to India before transitioning to "Resident and Ordinarily Resident" status
- **Caveats:** Deemed residency rule: Indian citizens with India-source income > INR 1.5M who are not liable to tax anywhere are "deemed residents" treated as NOR (still taxed on India-source income only)

### NRE Account (Non-Resident External Account)

- **Benefit:** Interest earned on NRE accounts is **fully exempt from income tax** in India for qualifying non-resident individuals (EY p. 607)
- **Eligibility:** Persons resident outside India under FEMA (exchange control laws), or those permitted by RBI to maintain NRE accounts
- **Caveats:** NRE interest ceases to be exempt when the individual returns to India and ceases to be a FEMA non-resident; timing needs careful management

### Public Provident Fund (PPF)

- **Benefit:** Under Normal Regime: contributions up to INR 150,000/year deductible (Section 80C); interest accrual and maturity proceeds fully tax-exempt (EEE — exempt-exempt-exempt)
- **Duration:** 15-year lock-in with partial withdrawals after 7 years; available to resident individuals
- **Caveats:** NRIs cannot open new PPF accounts but may continue existing accounts until maturity. Not available under Concessional Regime (80C deductions forfeited). (EY p. 614)

### Overseas Citizens of India (OCI)

- Multiple-entry, lifelong visa; OCI cardholders exempt from FRRO registration requirements; treated as foreign nationals for tax purposes if non-resident in India (EY pp. 633–634)
- From 1 January 2025, all PIO cards treated as OCI cards (PIO card invalid as travel document after 31 December 2024)

## 2026 Changes

- **Concessional regime is the DEFAULT from FY 2025-26:** From 1 April 2025, the new concessional regime is the default for all non-corporate taxpayers. Must actively opt into Normal (old) regime. (EY p. 618)
- **Revised concessional brackets (FY 2025-26):** Zero-rate band extended to INR 400,000; six progressive bands up to INR 2,400,000 before 30% applies. (EY p. 618)
- **Tax rebate raised under concessional regime:** Rebate up to INR 60,000 on income up to INR 1,200,000 — effectively 0% tax on income up to INR 1.2M. (EY p. 618)
- **Standard deduction:** INR 75,000 under concessional regime (effective 1 April 2024). (EY p. 606)
- **Long-term capital gains rate increased to 12.5%** (from 10%) on listed equity (effective 23 July 2024). Short-term capital gains on listed equity increased to 20% (from 15%). (EY p. 611)
- **Inflation indexation removed** for most assets transferred on/after 23 July 2024 (exception: land/building acquired before that date by resident individual). (EY p. 612)
- **TCS on foreign remittances from India:** From 1 April 2025, remittances ≥ INR 1 million subject to 20% TCS (5% for education/medical); from 1 January 2025, luxury goods > INR 1 million subject to 1% TCS. (EY p. 619)
- **PIO cards invalid:** From 31 December 2024, all PIO cards are invalid as travel documents; must be converted to OCI. (EY p. 633)

## Known Gaps

- [ ] **44ADA limit at €60k+:** Gross receipts of INR 5,400,000+ exceed the Section 44ADA limit of INR 5,000,000. At €60k and €100k, the presumptive regime is not available; standard regime with actual books applies. B2B figures above use 50% cost assumption as illustration only.
- [ ] **Normal regime comparison:** The examples show only the concessional regime. For high earners with large deductions (80C INR 150k + 80D INR 50k + NPS INR 50k + HRA), the Normal Regime may produce lower effective rates at middle income levels. Comparison table would be useful.
- [ ] **PPF under normal regime planning:** Returns to India with existing PPF and NRE accounts need careful timing analysis for the transition between FEMA non-resident status and income tax residency status.
- [ ] **Marginal relief at INR 5M surcharge threshold:** Not modeled; reduces effective tax where taxable income is slightly above INR 5M.
- [ ] **Deemed residency trap for NRIs:** Indian citizens with India-source income > INR 1.5M who are not liable to tax anywhere else are "deemed residents" — taxed on India-source income. Needs case-specific analysis.
- [ ] **GST compliance for freelancers:** Mandatory GST registration at INR 2,000,000 turnover for services; GST on professional services is 18%. This is a compliance burden but recoverable from B2B clients; net cost to individual depends on client ability to claim input credit.
- [ ] **EPF applicability for freelancers:** Self-employed/freelancers contracting through their own sole proprietorship have no mandatory EPF; those working through companies or large agencies may be covered — verify based on engagement structure.
