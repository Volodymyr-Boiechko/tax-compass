## Personal Income Tax

Lebanon taxes both residents and nonresidents on income derived in Lebanon. Certain categories exempt: agricultural workers, nurses, clergymen. Three schedular income categories: (1) sole trader profits, (2) salaries/wages/pensions, (3) movable capital income. Tax year = calendar year. Employers withhold PAYE monthly, remit every 3 months. Employees not required to file tax returns. (EY pp. 857–863)

**Important context:** Lebanon has been experiencing severe economic and currency crisis since 2019. The LBP has depreciated from ~LBP1,500/USD (pre-crisis) to over LBP100,000/USD (2024–2025). Tax brackets are regularly updated by Budget Laws. **All amounts below are in Lebanese Pounds (LBP).** Practical planning must account for LBP instability; effective rates on USD-equivalent income are frequently updated. (EY pp. 857–860)

### Deductions from Employment Income

- Representation allowances: up to **10% of basic salary**
- Transportation allowances (per Labor Law): deductible
- Schooling allowances (per Labor Law): deductible
- All allowances for disbursements incurred in performing employment duties (supported by invoices): deductible
- Per diems: max LBP50,000/day national; max LBP150,000/day international (EY p. 858)

### Investment Income

## Social Security

Lebanon operates a compulsory social security scheme administered by the National Social Security Fund (NSSF). Covers: sickness and maternity; family allowances; end-of-service indemnity. (EY pp. 863–864)

| Program | Employee | Employer | Salary ceiling (LBP/month) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sickness and maternity | **3%** | **8%** | LBP90,000,000/month |
| Family allowances | — | **6%** | LBP12,000,000/month |
| End-of-service indemnity | — | **8.5%** | No cap |
| **Employee total** | **3%** | | |
| **Employer total** | | **~22.5%** | |

**Note:** Family allowances ceiling: LBP12,000,000/month; sickness/maternity ceiling: LBP90,000,000/month.

**Non-Lebanese employees:** Not required to register with NSSF if their contracts are signed outside Lebanon with foreign entities and they can prove they enjoy equivalent social security benefits in their home country. (EY p. 863)

**Totalization agreements:** Lebanon has totalization agreements with Belgium, France, Italy, and the United Kingdom to prevent double SS contributions for expatriates. (EY p. 863)

## Self-Employment Regimes

### Option A — Sole Trader (Standard)

- **Who qualifies:** All sole traders, professionals, and individuals generating business income in Lebanon
- **Tax base:** Lump-sum profits = specified percentage of gross income (deemed profit basis); must submit total gross income statement to Ministry of Finance by 1 February each year
- **Rate / mechanism:** Progressive 4%–25% on net taxable income (after family exemptions) using sole trader brackets above
- **Nonresident sole traders:** Taxed on deemed profits at 17% effective rate:
  - Services: deemed profit = 50% of amounts received from Lebanon; effective tax = 17% × 50% = 8.5%
  - Products: deemed profit = 20% of amounts received; effective tax = 17% × 20% = 3.4%
  - Oil/gas sector services: effective tax = 10% (20% tax on 50% deemed profit); effective tax on products = 3% (20% tax on 15% deemed profit)
- **Quarterly payments:** Tax payable in four quarterly installments within 15 days after quarter-end
- **VAT obligations:** Lebanese VAT at 11%; registration required for taxable persons exceeding threshold

### Option B — No simplified regime

Lebanon has no separate presumptive or flat-rate regime for self-employed individuals beyond the deemed profit system for nonresidents. Resident sole traders use the standard brackets.

## Special Regimes for Newcomers / Expats

### No Dedicated Newcomer Tax Regime

Lebanon does not have a remittance basis, territorial tax, or new-resident personal income tax exemption.

### Non-Lebanese Employee NSSF Exemption

- Foreign employees with contracts signed outside Lebanon with foreign entities and equivalent foreign social security coverage are exempt from NSSF contributions
- This reduces the employer payroll cost burden for multinational assignments

### Dual Currency Context

- Since 2019, Lebanon operates a dual-currency economy (LBP and USD)
- Salaries are sometimes paid partly in USD; the LBP conversion rate used for tax purposes is the official Banque du Liban rate (which has been significantly below the parallel market rate during the crisis)
- Ministry of Finance Decision No. 959/1 provides the mechanism for computing tax on salaries paid partly in foreign currency (EY p. 860)

## 2026 Changes

- **Budget Law No. 324 rates effective from 1 January 2025:** Seven employment income brackets (2%–25%); annual single exemption LBP450,000,000. These remain in force for 2025 unless updated by subsequent Budget Law. (EY p. 859)
- **LBP exchange rate volatility:** The official rate has moved significantly; all LBP thresholds may be economically meaningless in USD terms depending on the rate applied. Planning must use current LBP/USD rate (verify at bdl.gov.lb).
- **Sole trader rates effective 1 January 2025:** Six-bracket structure (4%–25%) per Budget Law No. 324. (EY p. 859)
- **Nonresident withholding (from 1 April 2024):** Deemed profit for services = 50% of receipts; tax rate 17%; effective 8.5% WHT on nonresident service fees from Lebanon. (EY p. 859)

> **1 EUR ≈ LBP 100,000** (approximate parallel market rate, May 2026; official rate may differ — verify at bdl.gov.lb).
> **CRITICAL NOTE:** The LBP/EUR rate is highly uncertain. All calculations are illustrative. In practice, the economic burden depends on whether salary is paid in LBP or USD-equivalent.

**Working for €60,000 employment (LBP6,000,000,000):**
- Net taxable = 6,000M − 450M − 600M (10% representation) = 4,950,000,000
- Tax: 7,200,000 + 21,600,000 + 63,000,000 + 11% × (3,600M−1,800M) + 15% × (4,950M−3,600M) = 91,800,000 + 198,000,000 + 15% × 1,350,000,000 = 289,800,000 + 202,500,000 = **492,300,000**; NSSF capped = 32,400,000; total = **524,700,000; effective 8.7%**

**Key insight:** Lebanon's effective personal income tax rates are remarkably low — the progressive brackets are now calibrated to LBP amounts which represent small USD sums at the current exchange rate. At USD-equivalent €30,000 income, the effective income tax rate is only ~5–6%. This will change when/if brackets are further updated by Budget Laws, or if the exchange rate stabilizes. The low effective rates are a consequence of the economic crisis and inflation eroding the real value of LBP brackets.

## Known Gaps

- [ ] **LBP exchange rate uncertainty:** The primary risk in Lebanese tax planning. The official Banque du Liban rate vs. the Sayrafa platform rate vs. the parallel market rate produce vastly different USD-equivalent income amounts. Ministry of Finance uses specific conversion rates for tax computation on foreign currency salaries. Verify current official rate at bdl.gov.lb before any planning.
- [ ] **2026 Budget Law:** Lebanon typically enacts a Budget Law with updated tax brackets annually. The 2025 brackets (per Budget Law No. 324) are used above; a 2026 Budget Law may update all brackets significantly, especially given ongoing inflation. Verify current applicable rates.
- [ ] **Dual-currency salary treatment:** Many companies pay salaries partly in USD and partly in LBP. The Ministry of Finance mechanism for converting USD salary to LBP for tax purposes (Decision No. 959/1) involves specific conversion rates — not simply the market rate. Professional advice required for mixed-currency employment.
- [ ] **NSSF exemption for non-Lebanese:** Non-Lebanese employees with foreign contracts and equivalent home-country SS coverage may be exempt from Lebanese NSSF. This can reduce the effective employer cost significantly for multinational assignments.
- [ ] **Investment income WHT rates:** The current withholding tax rate on bank deposit interest in Lebanon has been subject to change; EY references historical 7% but the current applicable rate should be verified directly with the Lebanese tax authority.
- [ ] **CGT on real estate:** The 1% CGT on individual real estate sales is very low; the 15% rate applies to entities not otherwise subject to income tax. Expats owning Lebanese property should confirm which rate applies given their tax status.
- [ ] **Practical compliance:** Lebanon's tax administration has experienced significant disruption due to the economic crisis. Filing and payment procedures, banking system restrictions, and capital controls all affect practical tax compliance. Professional local advice is strongly recommended.
