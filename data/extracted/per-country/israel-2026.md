## Personal Income Tax

Israel imposes progressive income tax on worldwide income for residents; non-residents are taxed on Israel-source income only. Tax year = calendar year (1 January – 31 December). Tax is withheld monthly by employers; self-employed file annual returns. The Israeli tax system is based on self-assessment with withholding at source for employment income. No inheritance tax, no net worth tax. (EY pp. 685–697)

### Income Tax Brackets (2025 Annual — Employment / Self-employment)

| Taxable income (ILS) | Tax rate | Tax due (ILS) | Cumulative tax (ILS) |
|---|---|---|---|
| First 84,120 | **10%** | 8,412 | 8,412 |
| Next 36,600 (84,121–120,720) | **14%** | 5,124 | 13,536 |
| Next 73,080 (120,721–193,800) | **20%** | 14,616 | 28,152 |
| Next 75,480 (193,801–269,280) | **31%** | 23,399 | 51,551 |
| Next 291,000 (269,281–560,280) | **35%** | 101,850 | 153,401 |
| Next 161,280 (560,281–721,560) | **47%** | 75,802 | 229,203 |
| Above 721,560 | **50%** | — | — |

(EY p. 693)

**Important note on 47%/50% rates:** These rates apply to employment income and self-employment income derived by persons over 60. In other cases, a minimum tax rate of 31% applies to the first ILS269,280; 35% applies to income between ILS269,280 and ILS560,280; 47% applies to ILS560,280–ILS721,560; 50% applies above ILS721,560. (EY p. 693 footnote)

### High-Earner Surtax

- **3% surtax** is levied on total taxable income exceeding **ILS721,560** for 2025 (applied on top of the 50% rate → effective 53% marginal rate)
- From 1 January 2025: an additional **2% surtax** is levied on income above ILS721,560 derived from **passive/capital sources** (interest, dividends, capital gains) — in addition to the 3% surtax
- **Effective top marginal rate on passive/capital income above ILS721,560:** 50% + 3% + 2% = **55%** (for passive income above threshold)
- Surtax thresholds updated annually for inflation (EY p. 690)

### Tax Credits (Credit Points)

Israeli residents receive personal tax credits expressed as "credit points." Each credit point is worth **ILS242/month** (2025) = ILS2,904/year. Credit points are deducted from computed income tax. (EY p. 693)

| Category | Credit points |
|---|---|
| Unmarried male resident | 2.25 points |
| Unmarried female resident | 2.75 points |
| Each child under 18 | 1 point (female parent) + 0.5 (male) per child born/turning 18 |
| New immigrant (Oleh) — additional | For 42 months after Oleh status |

**Single male annual credit:** 2.25 × ILS242 × 12 = ILS6,534/year tax credit
**Single female annual credit:** 2.75 × ILS242 × 12 = ILS7,986/year tax credit

## Social Security (National Insurance — Bituach Leumi)

National insurance contributions are payable on taxable income for income tax purposes. For Israeli residents, rates include a supplementary health levy. (EY pp. 694–695)

### Israeli Residents (2025)

| Monthly income | Employer | Employee | Self-employed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Up to ILS7,522/month | 3.55% / 4.51% | 3.5% / 4.27% | 5.97% / 7.7% |
| ILS7,523 – ILS50,695/month | 7.60% | 12% / 12.7% | 17.83% / 19.99% |

Note: The two figures (e.g., 3.5%/4.27%) represent the breakdown between national insurance and health insurance. (EY p. 694)

### Nonresidents (2025)

| Monthly income | Employer | Employee |
|---|---|---|
| Up to ILS7,522/month | 0.59% / 0.75% | 0.04% / 0.1% |
| ILS7,523 – ILS50,695/month | 2.65% / 2.65% | 0.87% / 0.87% |

**Notes:**
- **Income ceiling:** ILS50,695/month (ILS608,340/year) — no contributions on income above this
- Self-employed: 52% of SS contributions paid during a tax year on unemployment income is deductible for income tax purposes
- Lower contributions may apply to Israeli residents who work abroad as self-employed for > 6 months, or for non-resident employers unless hired in Israel
- **No employer contributions on income above ILS50,695/month** (EY pp. 694–695)

## Self-Employment Regimes

### Option A — Standard Self-Employment (Osek Morshe / Osek Patur)

- **Who qualifies:** All self-employed individuals, freelancers, and independent contractors
- **Tax base:** Net business profit (gross revenue minus deductible business expenses); inflation adjustment rules apply for certain small businesses
- **Rate / mechanism:** Same progressive brackets as employment income (10%–50% + surcharges); tax paid via monthly/bimonthly prepayments and annual self-assessment return
- **Key deductions available:**
  - All business expenses (wholly and exclusively for business)
  - Payments to approved education/training funds: up to 4.5% of taxable income (annual limit ILS188,544 for 2025; ILS293,397 for self-employed)
  - Pension contributions: varying rates depending on age
- **Social security:** Self-employed pay Bituach Leumi at 5.97%/7.7% (up to ILS7,522/month) and 17.83%/19.99% (ILS7,522–ILS50,695/month); 52% of these contributions deductible for income tax purposes
- **VAT obligations:** Osek Morshe (registered dealer) must charge 17% VAT if annual turnover > ILS120,000; Osek Patur (exempt dealer) can opt out of VAT if turnover < ILS120,000 (subject to conditions)
- **Key limits:** Annual return due by 31 May (or by agreement); monthly advance payments; tax audit if income exceeds certain thresholds

### Option B — Closely Held Company (CHC) — note on limitations

- **Who qualifies:** 5 or fewer shareholders controlling the company
- **Recent changes (from 1 January 2025):** ITO Section 81 CHCs must now either: (a) pay a 2% annual surcharge on undistributed accumulated profits classified as "bad surpluses," or (b) distribute dividends totaling 6% of accumulated profits (5% if distributed during 2025 only). ITO Section 62A materially expanded to prevent CHCs from shielding personal service income from individual-level taxation
- **Practical effect:** CHC structures for freelancers/personal service companies now face stricter scrutiny; income from services provided by individual shareholders is taxed at personal rates
- **Note:** This option has become materially less attractive from 2025 due to legislative changes (EY pp. 688–689)

## Special Regimes for Newcomers / Expats

### New Immigrant / Olim Hadash (10-Year Foreign Income Exemption)

- **Eligibility:** "New Immigrants" (Oleh Hadash), First-Time Residents, or "Senior Returning Residents" (non-resident for ≥ 10 consecutive years)
- **Benefit:** **10-year exemption** from Israeli income tax on **all foreign-source income**, including: dividends, interest, rental income, capital gains from assets acquired or held during non-residency period, and **active employment income derived abroad** (including working for a foreign employer from abroad)
- **Israeli-source income:** Fully taxable under standard progressive rates; but New Immigrants receive **additional credit points** for 42 months after Oleh status
- **Option to defer residency:** May elect non-resident status for tax purposes during the first 12 months in Israel if special notice form submitted to ITA within 90 days of arrival
- **Application:** Obtain certificate of New Immigrant / Senior Returning Resident status from ITA for certainty; the certificate confirms eligibility for tax benefits
- **Duration:** 10 years from date of immigration/return (EY pp. 685–686)

### Upcoming 2026 changes for Olim:

- **From 1 January 2026:** Foreign income earned by New Immigrants, First-Time Residents, and Senior Returning Residents will remain **exempt from tax** if meeting exemption terms, but **must be reported to the Israeli Tax Authority** (reporting obligation introduced even when income is exempt)
- **2026 budget proposals for encouraging emigration to Israel:** Active income tax exemptions of NIS1,000,000 for 2026–2027 tax years; NIS600,000 for 2028; NIS300,000 for 2029; NIS150,000 for 2030 — specifically to encourage return of Israeli citizens and new immigrants (EY pp. 686–687)
- **Israeli-source income will be exempt for returning residents** (new from 2026 budget proposal)

### Senior Returning Residents (5-Year Passive Income Exemption)

- **Eligibility:** Israeli citizens who spent more than 6 years abroad
- **Benefit:** 5-year exemption on passive income (dividends, interest, rental income) earned abroad from assets purchased during non-residency; 10-year exemption on capital gains from such assets (EY p. 686)

### Foreign Expert (Expat Assignee)

- **Eligibility:** Foreign nationals legally authorized to work in Israel, working in their field of expertise, invited by a local Israeli entity, earning gross salary ≥ ILS14,800/month (2025)
- **Benefit:** For the first **12 months** in Israel: deduction for accommodation and living expenses up to ILS360/day (2025)
- **Foreign journalists and athletes:** Tax rate capped at 25% for the first 36 months (journalists) or 48 months (athletes), plus expat expense deductions (EY p. 693)

## 2026 Changes

- **2026 budget — Olim and returning residents:** Tax exemptions on active income up to NIS1,000,000 for 2026–2027; Israeli-source income to be exempt for returning residents; existing foreign-income exemption extended with new reporting requirement from 1 January 2026 (EY pp. 686–687)
- **Mandatory reporting of foreign income from 1 January 2026:** New Immigrants and Senior Returning Residents must report foreign income to ITA even if exempt (EY p. 686)
- **CHC regime tightened (effective 1 January 2025):** Section 81 surcharge on undistributed profits; Section 62A expanded to prevent shielding personal service income; major change for IT freelancers using Israeli holding companies (EY pp. 688–689)
- **High-earner surtax expanded (from 1 January 2025):** Additional 2% on passive/capital income above ILS721,560; combined with existing 3% → 5% total surtax on passive income above threshold (EY p. 690)
- **ITA residency definition regulations:** ITA still working to revise residency definition regulations toward end of 2025; updated information needed before taking action (EY p. 687)
- **War-related filing extensions:** Due to the ongoing conflict, extensions for 2024 and 2025 tax returns are expected to be available (EY p. 696)

## Known Gaps

- [ ] **Olim 10-year exemption at practice:** The 10-year foreign income exemption is extremely valuable for new immigrants with foreign assets/income. At €30k/€60k/€100k of entirely foreign-source income, the effective tax rate for a qualifying Oleh would be near-zero (only Israeli-source income taxed). This should be modeled separately for expat planning.
- [ ] **NIC rate structure complexity:** The EY table presents dual figures (e.g., 12%/12.7%) representing different NIC sub-components. The exact split between national insurance and health levy affects deductibility calculations; figures above are approximations.
- [ ] **CHC regime for freelancers:** The 2025 changes to ITO Section 62A and Section 81 significantly restrict the use of closely held companies to reduce personal tax. IT freelancers operating through Israeli companies need updated advice from local counsel.
- [ ] **2026 budget proposals still pending:** The Olim active income exemptions (NIS1M for 2026–2027) are budget proposals, not enacted law as of EY data date (October 2025). Verify enactment status.
- [ ] **Reporting obligation for Olim from 2026:** Even though foreign income remains exempt, it must be reported to ITA from 1 January 2026. This changes compliance obligations significantly for Olim with foreign assets.
- [ ] **Surtax threshold at €100k:** At ILS395,000 (~€100k), the income is well below the ILS721,560 surtax threshold; no 3% or 5% surtax applies. At ~€183k equivalent (ILS721,560), the surtax kicks in.
- [ ] **VAT rate:** Israel VAT is 17%; mandatory registration for annual turnover above ILS120,000 for self-employed. Not modeled above.
